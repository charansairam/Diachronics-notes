# Notes
1. **For the nouns which form their plural in *–k*/*–hk***, **the oblique marker seems to be *–t–* for singulars but *–n–* for plurals,** viz., *puṅgār–**t**–ōr* (> \**puṅgāṭōr*?) "flower{{sc:.ᴏʙʟ.ɢᴇɴ}}" but *puṅgāh–k–**n**–ōr* "flower{sc:..PL.OBL.GEN}}". This seems to also be the case for *cʰouwā* "a child", although its plural isn't ending in *–k* but *–g* (*cʰouwāṅg* "children"), e.g.: *cʰouwā–t–āl* "child{{sc:.OBL.ABL}}" but *cʰouwā(ṅ)–n–āl* "children{{sc:.OBL.ABL}}". If not that whole class of nouns it at least seems to be the case with *maṛā* "a cow", *lēṅg* "a voice", *allī* "a rat", *puṅgār* "a flower", *nāṅgēl* "a plough", *mēndul* "a body", *ār* "a woman", *rōn* "a house", *ḍōṅgur* "jungle", *mēs* "an egg", *nēl* "a field", *kurr* "a hen".
	- In Telugu, we have the retroflex oblique marker *–ṭ(i)*  as seen in *vāṭi* "{{sc:DIST.DEM.NHUM.PL.OBL}}" (*avi* "{{sc:DIST.DEM.NHUM.PL}}"), *iṇṭi* (< \**il⁽ⁿ⁾–t̠t̠–i(ṉ?)*) "house{{sc:.OBL}}" (*illu* "a house"), etc.; we have the dental oblique marker *–t(i)* as seen in *ṟāti* "stone{{sc:.OBL}}" (*ṟāyi* "a stone"), *nēti* "ghee{{sc:.OBL}}" (*nēyi/neyyi* "ghee"), etc., both of which are usually considered reflexes of alveolar\**–t̠t̠–iṉ* (for *–ṉ*, look at Old Tamil *avaṟṟiṉ* "{{sc:DIST.DEM.NHUM.PL.OBL}}", I am unsure whether this terminal *–ṉ* ever existed in the Gondi-Telugu branch — need to examine old Telugu texts for confirmation). We can assume that there is at least one common proto-Telugu-Gondi {{sc:OBL}}, i.e., *\*–t̠t̠–* .
2. In case of the *–r* ending word *tammur* "brother" we have the oblique form of the word as *tammū–n* for singular forms too ([[Gondi Grammar by HD Williamson.pdf#page=9&selection=243,0,271,5|Gondi Grammar by HD Williamson, page 9]])
